\begin{abstract}
When correlated measurements are naturally paired, through temporal repetition, matched subjects, or head-to-head competition, the variance of their difference depends on both the correlation structure and the relative magnitudes of the individual variances. Fisher's classical paired efficiency formula, $1/(1-\rho)$, captures this dependence only when variances are equal. We generalise this result to unequal variances through the Paired Efficiency Factor (PEF), $\eta = (1+\kappa)/(1+\kappa-2\sqrt{\kappa}\,\rho)$, where $\kappa$ denotes the variance ratio and $\rho$ the correlation coefficient. This formula is distribution-free and reduces to the classical case when $\kappa=1$.

Under a bivariate normal model, we derive a functional relationship between $\eta$ and the mutual information between the relative feature and a binary outcome. This relationship clarifies an apparent tension in machine learning practice: relative features can improve predictive performance even when $\eta<1$ (i.e., when relativisation increases variance), because information content and statistical efficiency measure distinct quantities. The two diverge most in competitive settings characterised by negative correlation and high variance asymmetry.

The primary empirical analysis uses key performance indicators (KPIs) from professional rugby union (United Rugby Championship ({URC}), $n=\PEFrugbyNgames$ games) and football (English Championship, $n=\PEFfootNgames$ games), where head-to-head competition provides natural pairing with both positive and negative correlation across KPIs. Supporting validation across four additional domains---pairwise oscillometric blood pressure readings from the {NHANES} examination component \parencite{nhanes2017pbxo}, paired transcript profiles from {GEO} series {GSE47462} \parencite{geoGSE47462,brunner2014earlybreast}, finance, and real industrial process telemetry ({OpenML} dataset 752; \parencite{openml752bosch})---shows domain-specific heterogeneity but overall alignment with the $(\kappa,\rho)$ taxonomy. The framework provides a four-quadrant guide for feature engineering decisions, supported by quadrant exemplars that demonstrate directional alignment between the $(\kappa,\rho)$ taxonomy and observed machine learning improvement when signal strength is controlled.




\end{abstract}
