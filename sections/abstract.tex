\begin{abstract}
Comparing two entities through the difference of their measurements is a basic operation across the quantitative sciences, from paired clinical trials and financial benchmarking to head-to-head competition. When the paired measurements are correlated, pairing is the classical route to sharper inference, and Fisher's paired-efficiency result, $1/(1-\rho)$, quantifies the gain in terms of the correlation $\rho$. However, this result assumes the two measurements share a common variance---an assumption routinely violated in competitive and asymmetric settings, where practitioners also encounter a paradox: relative (difference) features often improve prediction even when pairing demonstrably increases variance, with no unified account of when relativisation helps or harms. Here we show that a single distribution-free quantity resolves this tension: the Paired Efficiency Factor (PEF), $\eta = (1+\kappa)/(1+\kappa-2\sqrt{\kappa}\,\rho)$, where $\kappa$ is the variance ratio. The PEF recovers Fisher's classical formula when variances are equal ($\kappa=1$) and, through its link to predictive information, organises feature-engineering decisions.

Under a bivariate normal model we derive a closed-form relationship between $\eta$ and the mutual information between the relative feature and a binary outcome, establishing that statistical efficiency and information content are distinct quantities: relative features can raise predictive power even when $\eta<1$ (i.e.\ when relativisation increases variance), and the two diverge most under negative correlation with high variance asymmetry. The $(\kappa,\rho)$ plane partitions into four quadrants, each with distinct feature-engineering guidance. Using key performance indicators (KPIs) from professional rugby union (United Rugby Championship ({URC}), $n=\PEFrugbyNgames$ games) and football (English Championship, $n=\PEFfootNgames$ games), where head-to-head competition provides natural pairing across both positive and negative correlation, quadrant exemplars confirm that the taxonomy predicts the direction of machine-learning gain once signal strength is controlled. Validation across four further domains---pairwise oscillometric blood pressure readings from the {NHANES} examination component \parencite{nhanes2017pbxo}, paired transcript profiles from {GEO} series {GSE47462} \parencite{geoGSE47462,brunner2014earlybreast}, finance, and real industrial process telemetry ({OpenML} dataset 752; \parencite{openml752bosch})---shows domain-specific heterogeneity but overall alignment with the $(\kappa,\rho)$ taxonomy. These results recast established practices---market-adjusted returns, paired clinical designs, manufacturing control charts, and relative sports metrics---as instances of one correlation-based principle, and provide a practical, distribution-free diagnostic for deciding when to relativise features in competitive prediction.
\end{abstract}
