\begin{abstract}
Across finance, clinical medicine, and competitive sport, practitioners routinely choose between absolute measurements and quantities defined relative to a comparator. In rugby union and association football, relativising a key performance indicator (KPI)---replacing a team's count with the match-level difference from its opponent---sometimes sharpens match-outcome prediction and sometimes does not. The central question is therefore: \emph{why does relativisation benefit some KPIs but not others?} The answer depends on how strongly the two measurements correlate within each pairing unit---at match level in head-to-head team sport, and at the design-appropriate level in clinical, financial, and industrial settings. Under negative correlation the paradox sharpens: differencing can \emph{increase} variance, yet relative features still predict better in practice, with no unified account of when relativisation helps or harms. Fisher's classical paired-efficiency formula, $1/(1-\rho)$, explains variance reduction under positive correlation but assumes equal variances---an assumption routinely violated when competitors differ in variability and anti-correlate on high-volume counts. Here we show that a single distribution-free quantity resolves this tension: the Paired Efficiency Factor (PEF), $\eta = (1+\kappa)/(1+\kappa-2\sqrt{\kappa}\,\rho)$, where $\kappa$ is the variance ratio. The PEF recovers Fisher's result at $\kappa=1$ and, through its link to predictive information, organises feature-engineering decisions.

Under a bivariate normal model we derive a closed-form relationship between $\eta$ and the mutual information between the relative feature and a binary outcome, establishing that statistical efficiency and information content are distinct quantities: relative features can raise predictive power even when $\eta<1$, and the two diverge most under negative correlation with high variance asymmetry. The $(\kappa,\rho)$ plane partitions into four quadrants, each with distinct feature-engineering guidance. Using KPIs from professional rugby union (United Rugby Championship ({URC}), $n=\PEFrugbyNgames$ games) and football (English Championship, $n=\PEFfootNgames$ games), where head-to-head competition spans both positive and negative correlation, quadrant exemplars confirm that the taxonomy predicts the direction of machine-learning gain once signal strength is controlled. Validation across four further domains---pairwise oscillometric blood pressure readings from the {NHANES} examination component \parencite{nhanes2017pbxo}, paired transcript profiles from {GEO} series {GSE47462} \parencite{geoGSE47462,brunner2014earlybreast}, finance, and real industrial process telemetry ({OpenML} dataset 752; \parencite{openml752bosch})---shows domain-specific heterogeneity but overall alignment with the $(\kappa,\rho)$ taxonomy. These results recast market-adjusted returns, paired clinical designs, manufacturing control charts, and relative sports metrics as instances of one correlation-based principle, and provide a practical, distribution-free diagnostic for deciding when to relativise features in competitive prediction.
\end{abstract}
