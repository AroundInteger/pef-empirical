\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This study set out to establish, at the level of individual features, when and why relativisation improves outcome prediction, organised around four objectives (\cref{sec:methods}). Each is addressed by the results. First, the PEF--information content relationship (\cref{eq:mi_closed}) was confirmed on the idealised probit grid under (A1)--(A2), where $\eta$ and $I(X;Y)$ are strongly correlated at fixed signal strength. Second, the quadrant exemplars confirmed that the $(\kappa,\rho)$ taxonomy correctly predicts the direction of machine learning gain once $\delta/\sigma_{\mathrm{A}}$ is held comparable. Third, the Quadrant~4 results---positive ML improvement despite $\eta<1$---characterise the efficiency--power tension empirically. Fourth, the supporting domains demonstrate that the same $(\kappa,\rho)$ structure recurs across healthcare, clinical genomics, finance, and manufacturing.

The central consequence is that the absolute-versus-relative feature decision, currently settled by trial-and-error cross-validation, can instead be read off from three estimable quantities $(\kappa,\rho,\delta/\sigma_{\mathrm{A}})$: the framework converts feature engineering from search into prediction. We develop the theoretical implications, this practical payoff, and the limitations of these findings in turn.

\subsection{Theoretical Implications}

Statistical efficiency ($\eta$) and predictive power ($I(X;Y)$), though functionally related through \cref{eq:mi_closed}, measure distinct aspects of paired measurements. Classical variance-reduction reasoning, which advises against relativisation when $\eta<1$, addresses only one dimension of feature engineering decisions because it does not account for information content. Given estimates of $\kappa$, $\rho$, and $\delta$, practitioners can compute both $\eta$ and $I(X;Y)$ and make informed decisions accordingly.

The distribution-free PEF formula (\cref{eq:pef}) holds for any paired measurements with finite second moments. The information content relationship (\cref{eq:mi_closed}) requires the stronger assumption of bivariate normality (A1). This distinction is important: the efficiency characterisation is broadly applicable, whilst the predictive-power analysis is contingent on distributional form.

This analysis deliberately operates at the level of individual KPIs. At the single-KPI level, the mechanism is established: $\eta$ and $I(X;Y)$ together identify the theoretical regime, and the quadrant exemplars confirm that directional predictions hold in practice (\cref{sec:exemplars}). This single-KPI foundation is a prerequisite for any principled extension to the multivariate case, and provides a diagnostic methodology for studies concerned with the predictability of match-level outcomes: compute $(\kappa,\rho)$ for each candidate KPI, classify its quadrant, then assess whether the information structure justifies relativisation.

The prior studies that motivated this work \parencite{bennett2019descriptive,bennett2021predicting,scott2023urc,scott2023womens} used multi-KPI models, where inter-feature correlations and joint classification boundaries introduce additional structure not captured by univariate PEF. How per-feature gains compound, cancel, or interact when multiple relativised KPIs are combined in a single classifier remains an open question. Multivariate extensions that characterise joint efficiency across feature sets are a natural next step, building on the per-KPI regime characterisation established here.

\subsection{Cross-Domain Unification}

Practices that appear domain-specific, market-adjusted returns, paired clinical designs, control charts, relative sports metrics, can be understood as instances of correlation-based variance reduction with different $(\kappa,\rho)$ values. Where two domains share similar parameter structures, methods from one may apply to the other. Analogous relativisation appears in education, where value-added formulations benchmark teacher performance against comparable cohorts \parencite{hanushek2010}.

The framework also accounts for why certain practices succeed in some domains but not others. Healthcare (Quadrant~2, high $\rho$) consistently benefits from pairing. Sports KPIs span all four quadrants, with the balance between regimes depending on the specific KPI type: kicking and set-piece KPIs tend toward positive correlation (Quadrant~1--2), whilst defensive and discipline KPIs sit predominantly in Quadrants~3--4, requiring explicit analysis of both $\eta$ and $I(X;Y)$ to guide relativisation decisions.

\subsection{Practical Guidance}

The framework suggests the following procedure:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Estimate $\kappa$ and $\rho$ from available data.
  \item Compute $\eta$ using \cref{eq:pef}.
  \item Identify the quadrant from \cref{tab:quadrants}.
  \item For Quadrants~1--2, use relative features (they improve both statistical efficiency and information content).
  \item For Quadrant~3, prefer absolute features.
  \item For Quadrant~4, additionally estimate $\delta/\sigma_{\mathrm{A}}$ and compute $I(X;Y)$ using \cref{eq:mi_closed,eq:delta_ratio} to determine whether information content justifies relativisation despite $\eta<1$.
\end{enumerate}
This reduces reliance on trial-and-error cross-validation to determine feature strategy; the decision logic is fully determined by $(\kappa,\rho,\delta/\sigma_{\mathrm{A}})$. The wider significance is twofold. For practitioners, it converts feature engineering from search into prediction: the relative-versus-absolute decision becomes a calculation from estimable quantities rather than an empirical sweep. For the research literature on the predictability of competitive outcomes---where debate persists over how much of a result is skill versus chance---the framework offers a principled, per-feature account of which measurements carry decision-relevant information and why.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Distributional assumptions.} The PEF formula is distribution-free, but the information content relationship (\cref{eq:mi_closed}) assumes bivariate normality. Whilst the central limit theorem provides some robustness, strongly skewed or multimodal distributions may require non-parametric extensions. For count-based KPIs, the transformation $Z=\log(X+1)$ can improve normality whilst preserving the $(\kappa,\rho)$ structure: across 24 rugby KPIs the rank ordering by $\rho$ was unchanged after transformation (Spearman $r_{s}=0.997$) and the aggregate PEF change was negligible ($\bar{\eta}_{\log}/\bar{\eta}=1.007$, $p=0.712$; Supplementary~Note~S1), though individual count-based KPIs (rucks won, kick metres, kicks from hand) showed improvements of 19--37\%. Systematic characterisation of the skewness threshold beyond which variance-stabilising transforms become necessary remains an active area of investigation.

\textbf{Stationarity.} The framework treats $\kappa$ and $\rho$ as fixed parameters, yet they may vary across time, conditions, or subpopulations. Temporal instability in correlation structure, regime changes in financial markets, seasonal effects in sports, could reduce prediction reliability if parameters are estimated from one period and applied to another.

\textbf{Sample size requirements.} Reliable estimation of $\kappa$ and $\rho$ requires sufficient observations. The framework's mathematical correctness holds for any parameter values, but empirical estimation imposes practical constraints on minimum sample sizes.

\textbf{Binary outcomes.} The information content analysis addresses binary classification (predicting which entity outperforms). Extensions to multi-class classification, regression, and more complex prediction tasks are not addressed.

\textbf{Strategic interactions.} In competitive contexts, entities may adjust behaviour in response to opponents, potentially altering correlation structures dynamically. The current framework does not model such strategic adaptations.

\textbf{Primary evidence base.} The detailed empirical analysis is drawn primarily from rugby union and football KPIs. Whilst supporting validation from healthcare, genomics, finance, and manufacturing is consistent with the framework's predictions, these domains have not been subjected to the same depth of analysis. Further dedicated studies in each domain would strengthen the evidence for cross-domain applicability.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Several extensions are worth investigating:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Non-parametric PEF.} Rank-based correlation measures and permutation-based inference could yield distribution-free analogues of the information content relationship.
  \item \textbf{Multivariate generalisation.} PEF matrices characterising joint efficiency of multiple correlated feature pairs.
  \item \textbf{Time-varying PEF.} Incorporating temporal dynamics through rolling-window or state-space estimation.
  \item \textbf{Causal inference connections.} Clarifying when correlation-based variance reduction preserves or distorts causal estimates.
  \item \textbf{Additional domain validation.} Extending to social sciences, environmental studies, and engineering systems.
\end{itemize}
